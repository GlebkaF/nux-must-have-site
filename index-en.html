How to build your own presets with GPT

ChatGPT can be a powerful partner for tone‑tweaking. Here’s a simple workflow to design your own sound:

  1. Grab our preset pack as a starting point and explore the JSON structure – each block defines a noise gate, effect, amp, cabinet and so on.
  2. Open a chat with ChatGPT and describe the style you want: for example, “build a crunchy blues preset with a Fender Twin and a Tube Screamer.”
  3. ChatGPT will suggest a chain with settings. Adjust them to taste – start with the amp and IR, then tweak EQ and effects.
  4. Save the output as a JSON file and import it into Mightier Amp (use the import button in the library). You can also generate a QR code from the JSON using online tools.

FAQ & Insights

Why do I need multiple presets?

Different musical styles demand different tone settings. A high‑gain metal sound will feel muddy for clean arpeggios, while a pristine clean will lack the sustain and saturation needed for solos. Having specialised presets lets you switch tones quickly without re‑dialing everything.

How do I choose the right IR and set the low/high cut?

Start with a cabinet model that matches your amp choice (e.g. a 1960AV for a Marshall tone or a JZ120 for a clean jazz sound). Apply a low‑cut around 70–90 Hz to remove rumble and a high‑cut between 8–12 kHz to tame excessive fizz. Adjust by ear until the palm‑mutes are tight and the treble doesn’t hiss.

What’s the difference between JCM800 and JCM2000?

The JCM800 is a single‑channel amp from the 1980s with a raw, punchy character and requires a pedal boost for modern high‑gain. The JCM2000, introduced in the late 1990s, has multiple channels, more gain and built‑in compression, making it more versatile but slightly smoother and less aggressive.

What does “tight” mean in metal tone?

A tight tone responds quickly to picking and has controlled low frequencies. You achieve it by using a boost pedal to cut flabby bass, setting the amp’s bass lower and applying a noise gate. It helps palm‑mutes stay percussive and stops the sound from “flopping.”

Why do rhythm and lead presets use different reverb and delay?

Riffs and rhythm parts need to be clear and punchy, so we keep reverb short (room or plate) and sometimes skip delay. Lead lines benefit from extra sustain and ambience, so we add a hall reverb and a moderate delay to fill the space without muddying the timing.
